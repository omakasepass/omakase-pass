What is Omakase?

Omakase is a Japanese word that means, roughly, "I leave it up to you." When a guest sits at a sushi counter and says it, they are handing the chef the whole shape of the meal: no menu, no ordering, no bill known in advance. In its place is trust: the belief that the person across the counter will choose better than you could, and will spend that trust wisely.

The form has deep roots. In the Edo period (1603–1868), sushi began as fast, hand-formed street food sold from carts in the city then called Edo, today's Tokyo. Fish was pressed onto seasoned rice and eaten in a bite or two. What we now recognize as the refined counter experience, a chef working directly in front of a seated guest, course by course, grew out of that tradition over the following century, as sushi moved indoors and the relationship between chef and diner became the point of the meal, not just the food.

Omakase as a named, deliberate way of dining spread widely in the 1990s, when a generation of chefs, in Japan and increasingly abroad, began building entire evenings around it. The idea traveled well because it answered something a printed menu never could: it made every meal specific to that day's catch, that season, and that room of guests.

At the heart of it sits an older idea still, borrowed from the tea ceremony: ichi-go ichi-e, "one time, one meeting." It holds that every gathering is unrepeatable: this fish, these people, this evening will never align in exactly this way again, and so it deserves the full attention of everyone present. That is the real covenant of omakase. Not luxury, and not spectacle, but care spent on a moment precisely because the moment will not come back.

It's that covenant we're building The Omakase Pass around, and bringing to tables across Nashville.

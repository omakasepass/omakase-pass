What is Omakase?

Omakase is a Japanese word that means, roughly, "I leave it up to you." When a guest sits at a sushi counter and says it, they are handing the chef the whole shape of the meal: no menu, no ordering, no bill known in advance. In its place is trust: the belief that the person across the counter will choose better than you could, and will spend that trust wisely.

The form has deep roots. In the Edo period (1603–1868), sushi began as fast, hand-formed street food sold from carts in the city then called Edo, today's Tokyo. Fish was pressed onto seasoned rice and eaten in a bite or two. What we now recognize as the refined counter experience, a chef working directly in front of a seated guest, course by course, grew out of that tradition over the following century, as sushi moved indoors and the relationship between chef and diner became the point of the meal, not just the food.

Omakase as a named, deliberate way of dining spread widely in the 1990s, when a generation of chefs, in Japan and increasingly abroad, began building entire evenings around it. The idea traveled well because it answered something a printed menu never could: it made every meal specific to that day's catch, that season, and that room of guests.

At the heart of it sits an older idea still, borrowed from the tea ceremony: ichi-go ichi-e, "one time, one meeting." It holds that every gathering is unrepeatable: this fish, these people, this evening will never align in exactly this way again, and so it deserves the full attention of everyone present. That is the real covenant of omakase. Not luxury, and not spectacle, but care spent on a moment precisely because the moment will not come back.

It's that covenant we're building The Omakase Pass around, and bringing to tables across Nashville.

How an omakase dinner works

There is no menu to read and nothing to order. You sit, usually at a counter or a table set for the occasion, and the chef begins. Courses arrive one at a time, each a single considered bite or a small plate, handed to you at the moment it is meant to be eaten. The sequence is deliberate: leaner fish and lighter flavors first, building toward the richer and more pronounced, with the pace set by the chef reading the room. You eat with your hands or with chopsticks, whichever the piece calls for. The meal ends not at a fixed number of courses but when the chef decides it is complete. Your only real job is to arrive hungry and to trust the person feeding you.

Omakase vs. ordering off a sushi menu

At most sushi restaurants you order: you scan a menu, choose your rolls and nigiri, and the kitchen makes what you asked for. Omakase inverts that. You give up the choosing, and in return the chef gives you their best judgment: the fish they sourced that morning, prepared the way they believe it should be eaten. It usually costs more, because it is built around quality and attention rather than volume, and because much of what you are paying for is the chef's time and taste. What you get is a meal that could never have come off a menu, specific to the day, the season, and the people at the table.

What a private omakase at home is like

A private omakase keeps everything that makes the counter special and removes the restaurant around it. A vetted chef comes to your home, sets up, and serves the same course-by-course experience at your own table, for your own people. There is no reservation to chase and no dining room to share. It is the same covenant, ichi-go ichi-e, one evening made once, in the one place that is entirely yours.
